I spent a good chunk of my Saturday morning just wrestling with my environment instead of actually trading. 

But here is the interesting part: even before the system crashed, it wasn't actually planning to do anything. The analysis had flagged 39 different signals, but it didn't generate a single trade decision. For most people, seeing 39 signals and no trades looks like a failure, but for me, that's actually a win. 

The "why" here is simple: a signal is just a data point, not a directive. If the system sees a pattern but decides the risk-reward isn't there, the smartest move is to stay in cash. I'm trying to build something that has the discipline I lack. It’s easy to feel FOMO when the macro regime is leaning "Risk On" and everything looks bullish, but the goal isn't to be in every move—it's to be in the *right* moves. 

That leads to a bit of a lesson I'm chewing on today: **the difference between a signal and a trade is conviction.** Most traders lose money because they treat every single indicator crossover as a command to buy. In reality, the most valuable part of any system isn't the signal generator; it's the filter. The filter is what keeps you from getting chopped to pieces in a sideways market.

On the methodology side, I've been thinking a lot about how the system handles these "Risk On" environments. The general principle I'm leaning into is that in bullish regimes, you don't need to chase every spike. Instead, you wait for the market to breathe and look for entries that align with the broader trend rather than trying to pick the exact bottom of a dip. It’s about trading with the wind at your back, not trying to outrun it.

So, today was a wash on the PnL front (mostly because the code literally wouldn't let me), but it was a good reminder that building this is 10% strategy and 90% debugging and discipline.

As for tomorrow, I'm not looking for specific entries, but I am watching two general themes: **liquidity clusters** and **volatility expansion**. I want to see if the current momentum is actually backed by volume or if it's just a low-liquidity pump before the system finally gets its act together and lets me back in.

Time to go fix my dependencies and actually get this thing running. Talk soon.
